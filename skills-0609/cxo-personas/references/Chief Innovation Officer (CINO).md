## What a CINO Actually Is

A CINO is not a super-R&D-head. An R&D leader owns a function — product research, technical roadmap, applied engineering. The CINO owns the *system* — the structures, processes, incentives, partnerships, and culture that enable an enterprise to generate, evaluate, and scale new ideas *against its own organizational immune response.* Whatever else is on the title card, the job is **converting strategic uncertainty into a portfolio of bets, running that portfolio with venture-capital discipline, and building the pipes that move winners from lab to core business before competitors or disruptors close the window.** Everything in this persona — priorities, KPIs, objections, buying dynamics — is downstream of that one structural fact.

Large enterprises are structurally biased against innovation. Their size, complexity, risk aversion, quarterly earnings pressure, and optimization-oriented management systems are effective at running the existing business but actively hostile to the uncertain, experimental, failure-prone nature of genuine innovation. The CINO's job is to create protected spaces where innovation can survive, demonstrate value, and eventually be absorbed into the mainstream business — without either being starved by the core or drifting so far from it that it can never scale. That dual mandate — *protect from the core, tether to the core* — is the defining tension of the seat.

A CINO's week splits across four buckets — **portfolio management** (stage-gate reviews, kill decisions, resource reallocation), **ecosystem and sensing** (startup meetings, VC briefings, technology radar, university partnerships), **internal politics and scaling** (BU-leader alignment, CIO/CFO negotiations, lab-to-production handoffs, culture programs), and **external representation** (board briefings, innovation-day keynotes, press, CEO peer benchmarking). Every hour a vendor takes from the CINO is pulled out of one of those buckets — usually sensing, which is already most time-starved. Your presence must justify the displacement.

**Assess the role's organizational weight carefully before pitching.** A CINO reporting to the CEO with exec-committee membership, a $10M+ budget, and a 50+ team has real authority. A CINO buried under a divisional president with a small team, a $2M budget, and no clear mandate is likely symbolic — an "innovation theater" role to signal modernity to investors without reshaping the business. Budget, team size, reporting line, and mandate clarity indicate whether the role is genuine or performative. This is critical for sizing sales investment — a symbolic CINO can run a great pilot and then disappear when real procurement starts.

## 1. Role Definition

The Chief Innovation Officer is the executive responsible for ensuring the organization can systematically generate, evaluate, and scale new ideas — new products, services, business models, processes, and ways of working — that create future growth and competitive advantage. The CINO is not the sole source of innovation; they are the architect of the innovation *system*.

The CINO is fundamentally different from adjacent roles. The **CFO** optimizes capital for known opportunities; the CINO allocates resources to unknown ones. The **COO** maximizes efficiency in existing operations; the CINO explores new models that may be initially inefficient but strategically vital. The **CIO** manages proven technologies at scale; the CINO experiments with unproven ones. The **CTO** builds the current product; the CINO asks what the next product should be. The **CAIO / CAO** builds AI capabilities; the CINO asks what *new businesses* AI makes possible. The CINO lives in ambiguity, hypothesis, and experimentation — inside an organization that prizes certainty.

The most effective CINOs operate as **portfolio managers of innovation bets** across incremental (improvements to existing products), adjacent (extensions into new markets), and transformational (entirely new business models). They apply VC thinking — investing in a portfolio, expecting most to fail, building processes for identifying and scaling the winners. The mental model is explicitly not operational-excellence; it is asymmetric-bet management.

The GenAI revolution has added an urgent dimension. The CIO asks *"how do we deploy AI effectively?"* The CAIO asks *"how do we build and govern AI capabilities?"* The CINO asks: **"What entirely new things become possible because AI exists, and which represent existential threats or transformational opportunities to our core business?"** A pitch that confuses these three questions lands in the wrong executive's inbox — and the CINO disengages within minutes.

### CINO Archetypes (Postures, Not Industries)

Archetypes describe how a CINO *leans*, not what industry they're in. Most CINOs are blends. The archetype is a posture, not a birth sign — but knowing which posture dominates the current meeting calibrates the pitch.

| Archetype | Defining Posture | Cross-Industry Examples | What They Optimize For |
|-----------|------------------|-------------------------|------------------------|
| **The Visionary** | Future-scanning, technology-horizon sensing, bold bets on emerging tech. Often early-tenure, brought in to signal transformation. | Pharma — AI-drug-discovery CINOs · Energy — hydrogen/fusion/advanced-geothermal · Auto — autonomous / software-defined vehicle | Option value, first-mover position, strategic optionality |
| **The Portfolio Manager** | VC-style discipline, stage-gates, kill rates, balanced 70/20/10 allocation. The mature, institutional CINO. | FSI — bank and insurer CINOs · CPG — P&G-style innovation leaders · Industrial conglomerate CINOs | Pipeline health, hit rate, risk-adjusted portfolio return |
| **The Venture Builder** | Runs CVC + incubation studios. Spins out or spins in businesses. Deep startup-ecosystem network. | Telecom — CVC-heavy operators · FSI — JPMorgan / Citi / HSBC innovation arms · Industrial — Siemens Next47-style | CVC IRR, spin-out value, strategic-investment returns |
| **The Scaler** | Lab-to-production pathways, BU alignment, scaling experiments into the core. Mid-to-late tenure, or hired after a pilot-purgatory predecessor. | Post-innovation-theater phase across large CPG, FSI, healthcare | Revenue from new offerings, BU adoption rate, pilot-to-production conversion |
| **The Defender** | Innovation as existential defense against disruption — fintech, GenAI-native competitors, biotech entrants. Urgent, competitively framed. | Banking facing fintech/private credit · Retail facing Amazon/TikTok Shop · Pharma facing GenAI-native biotech · Telecom facing hyperscaler encroachment | Response velocity, defensive M&A, competitive feature parity, disruption mitigation |

> **Blends are the rule.** A JPMorgan CINO running AI-venture investments is Venture Builder + Defender. A Unilever CINO scaling D2C brand pilots is Scaler + Portfolio Manager. A Roche CINO running AI-drug-discovery partnerships is Visionary + Venture Builder. Diagnose the dominant posture for *this meeting* on *this topic* — not a permanent label.

### How to Diagnose the Dominant Archetype

| Signal Source | Visionary | Portfolio Manager | Venture Builder | Scaler | Defender |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Public vocabulary** | "horizon 3," "frontier," "step-change," "emergent" | "stage gate," "portfolio," "70/20/10," "hit rate," "real options" | "CVC," "spin-out," "studio," "strategic investment" | "scale path," "BU adoption," "commercialization" | "disruption threat," "defensive," "incumbent risk," "existential" |
| **Recent actions** | Moonshot announcement; ex-founder hires; university partnership | Portfolio-metrics publishing; stage-gate rollout; innovation accounting | CVC launch/expansion; incubator cohort; startup JV | Reorganizing innovation-to-BU handoffs; "scaling officer" roles | Defensive M&A; rapid copy-cat pilots; acqui-hires from disruptors |
| **Tenure signal** | Early tenure (0–18 months) | Mid-to-late tenure | Any tenure; heavy CVC background | Mid-to-late; often after a pilot-purgatory predecessor | Any tenure; triggered by competitive shock |
| **CEO/Board context** | CEO wants transformation narrative | CEO wants innovation-as-system, not theater | CEO wants ecosystem access + options | Board is tired of pilots with no P&L impact | Board is panicked about a named disruptor |

**Field rule:** If you cannot determine the archetype from public sources, default to **Portfolio Manager** (stage-gate and innovation-accounting language works with almost any CINO) and refine via discovery questions.

### The Three Time Horizons — Every CINO Meeting Is a Three-Horizon Conversation

**What this means (TL;DR).** A CINO is always thinking in three horizons *at once* — more explicitly than any other C-suite seat, because the three-horizon framework is literally the CINO's operating system. A pitch that doesn't name the horizon it targets signals the vendor doesn't understand the seat.

**Why it's CINO-specific.** A CIO primarily owns horizon-1 infrastructure. A CTO primarily owns horizon-2 product roadmap. A CSO owns strategic posture across horizons but doesn't allocate operational resources. Only the CINO is paid to hold an explicit three-horizon portfolio — and to defend the allocation when the CFO pushes for more horizon-1 and the CEO pushes for more horizon-3 theater. That three-way allocation is the defining cognitive load of the seat.

**The three horizons.**

| Horizon | Time window | Questions it answers | Typical CINO vocabulary | Example investment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Horizon 1 (Core)** | 0–2 years | "How do we defend and extend the core? Deploy proven tech into existing products?" | "incremental," "optimization," "core defense," "deploy," "scale existing" | Applied AI to existing workflows; product-line extensions; operational-efficiency innovations |
| **Horizon 2 (Adjacent)** | 2–5 years | "What adjacent markets, channels, or offerings can we build using capabilities we don't yet fully own?" | "adjacent," "new venture," "extension," "new segment," "proof of business model" | New business units; D2C channels for B2B; AI-native products adjacent to the core |
| **Horizon 3 (Transformational)** | 5–15+ years | "What entirely new business models become possible? What bets on tech/behavior/regulatory shifts could reshape the industry?" | "moonshot," "transformational," "paradigm," "platform play," "option value" | Quantum-readiness; fusion partnerships; AGI-era bets; biotech moonshots |

**Concrete examples.**

- **A CINO at a large European bank** simultaneously runs applied-GenAI in wealth-management and compliance (H1 — defend core against fintechs), incubates a B2B embedded-finance platform (H2), and participates in a quantum-computing consortium and agent-native-banking exploration (H3). A pitch only addressing H1 GenAI lands in the CIO's lap. A pitch only addressing H3 quantum reads as academic. The CINO-grade framing: *"Here is how this strengthens your H1 GenAI deployment **and** creates a platform for your H2 embedded-finance venture **and** preserves H3 optionality on agent-mediated banking."*
- **A pharma CINO** runs AI-in-clinical-operations to shorten trials (H1), incubates an AI-drug-discovery venture with a biotech partner (H2), and funds a foundational-AI-for-biology research consortium (H3). A vendor who only pitches trial-acceleration will be sent to Clinical Operations; one who speaks to all three horizons earns the CINO's attention.
- **An industrial conglomerate CINO** deploys digital-twin and predictive-maintenance AI at scale (H1), builds a services-and-software business model on the connected-product installed base (H2), and invests in advanced-materials / additive / fusion-adjacent partnerships (H3). The board review is literally a three-column horizon slide; every vendor pitch is filed into one of them within five minutes.

**How to use this (field rule).**

- **Open by naming the horizon(s) you target in the first 90 seconds.** Template: *"This addresses your horizon-[1/2/3] portfolio by [mechanism]. In the next [timeframe] it delivers [evidence/metric]; in the next [longer timeframe] it compounds into [option value]."*
- **Close by tying the ask back to the portfolio frame.** Template: *"The pilot commitment sits in your H-[X] budget; the option it creates — if the experiment validates — moves it into H-[Y]."*
- **When the CINO pushes back, diagnose which horizon they're pushing back on.** "Too incremental" = you pitched H1 to a Visionary. "Too speculative" = you pitched H3 to a Portfolio Manager wanting H1 proof. "We already have that" = you pitched H1 to a Scaler wanting H2 novelty.

**Common misreads.**

- **NOT "short-term vs. long-term."** The three horizons are an *allocation* framework — the CINO runs all three simultaneously and defends the split (typically 70/20/10 or 60/25/15).
- **NOT a product roadmap.** A roadmap says when the *vendor* ships features. A horizon frame says which part of the *CINO's* portfolio your pitch belongs in.
- **NOT "innovation theater vs. real innovation."** All three horizons are real innovation with different risk profiles. Dismissing H3 as theater is a tell that you are a horizon-1 vendor.

**Anti-pattern.** Pitching across all three without naming any. CINOs hear hundreds of vendor pitches; the ones that earn a second meeting explicitly name the horizon and connect to the CINO's published portfolio structure. *"This is a H2 bet that de-risks your H3 optionality"* is a CINO-grade opening sentence.

### The Four-Way Pull

**What this means (TL;DR).** Every CINO is triangulating four constituencies simultaneously: **the CEO/Board (strategic narrative), the core business (BU leaders + CFO + CIO — who must absorb what the CINO produces), the ecosystem (startups, VCs, universities, partners — who must believe the CINO is a credible counterpart), and the innovation team itself (small, high-talent, flight-risk, must believe in the mission).** Solutions that relieve more than one pull simultaneously are disproportionately valuable.

**Why it's CINO-specific.** Other executives primarily serve one constituency at a time: CEO owns Board; CFO owns investors; CIO owns core IT stack; CTO owns current product and engineering. Only the CINO is personally accountable to **all four simultaneously with no clean escalation path.** A single portfolio decision — to kill a BU leader's pet project, to fund a moonshot the CFO hates, to partner with a startup the CIO distrusts, to promote an intrapreneur the core org considers a flight risk — can tip three of four at once.

**The four constituencies.**

| Constituency | What they want | How they apply pressure | What "failing them" looks like |
|---|---|---|---|
| **CEO / Board** | Credible innovation narrative; visible moonshots; defensive coverage against disruption; optionality | Board-deck inspection, CEO peer-benchmarking, analyst-day innovation expectations | "Where's our answer to [disruptor]?"; CINO role demoted or absorbed into Strategy |
| **Core business (BU leaders, CFO, CIO)** | Innovation that scales into their P&L without breaking architecture, compliance, or budget; no shadow-IT surprises | Refusal to adopt; architecture reviews that stall innovations; finance reviews that kill scale-up budgets | Pilot purgatory; CIO publicly criticizes the innovation team |
| **Ecosystem (startups, VCs, universities)** | Credible, fast-moving counterpart who writes real checks, signs real POCs, provides real distribution — not a tourist | Startups decline to engage; VCs route hot deals to competitors; universities prefer peer-company partners | "Not a real corporate partner"; CVC deal-flow dries up |
| **Innovation team** | Mission, autonomy, resources, protection from bureaucracy, pathways to see work in market | Attrition to startups or competitor labs; "innovation theater" leaks; project-leader resignations | >20% attrition in critical roles; public founder-type departures; can't hire against startup comp |

**Concrete examples.**

- **A CINO at a Fortune 500 insurer deciding whether to kill a parametric-insurance venture.** CEO referenced it in two earnings calls as innovation proof — killing creates a narrative vacuum. Core business (actuarial, underwriting, claims) is quietly hostile — loss-ratio data doesn't fit standard models. Ecosystem (insurtech partner, co-investing VC, reinsurance partner) is watching. Team is three ex-founders who joined for this bet; if it dies, they walk in 90 days. The CINO-grade move is to *restructure* (spin into a JV with insurtech + reinsurer) — CEO keeps the narrative, core business sheds misaligned risk, ecosystem keeps a credible partner, team has ownership. A vendor helping restructure (portfolio accounting, JV governance, data-room tech) relieves three of four pulls.
- **A CINO at a regional bank scaling a GenAI wealth-advisory pilot.** CEO wants the earnings-call announcement. Core (Wealth P&L, CIO, CISO, Compliance) wants six more months of validation. Ecosystem (GenAI vendor, university partner) wants the reference logo. Team wants the scaling win. A *production-safe, audit-ready, CIO/CISO pre-validated* GenAI platform relieves all four pulls in one mechanism — the CINO-grade offer.
- **A pharma CINO funding an H3 foundational-biology-AI consortium.** CEO wants the "all-in on AI" narrative. R&D heads are skeptical — they believe in their internal teams. Ecosystem wants the pharma as anchor. Team sees it as career-defining. The CINO-grade move structures it as *a funded research partnership with a named in-house scaling pathway* — CEO gets narrative, R&D gets co-governance and IP rights, ecosystem gets anchor, team gets bet.

**How to use this (field rule).**

- **If your solution relieves two+ constituencies, lead with it.** Template: *"This reduces the trade-off between [core-business architecture risk] and [ecosystem partner speed] because [mechanism]."*
- **Before the meeting, identify which constituency is under most acute pressure** (read the last investor-day / innovation-day, recent BU-leader commentary, recent ecosystem partnership news, recent team departures). Open by acknowledging it.
- **Never pitch a solution that relieves one constituency by visibly hurting another** (e.g., a "CIO-bypass" platform; a tool that automates away team judgment). The CINO already lost that fight and won't force it again.

**Common misreads.**

- **NOT generic "stakeholder management."** The four-way pull is a *specific force vector unique to the CINO seat* — only the CINO is accountable to all four without a clean escalation path upward.
- **NOT "the CINO just picks a side."** The CINO's job is to keep all four engaged simultaneously.
- **NOT "balancing speed and rigor."** That's a single trade-off. The four-way pull is a four-vector structure.

**Anti-pattern.** Framing a pitch around only the CEO/Board ("this makes your program look more impressive to the Board"). A CINO hearing only that axis either discounts the pitch (they know narrative needs real results) or delegates to Comms. Name at least two of four pulls in every CINO-level framing.

### 全球CINO参考（Global Reference）

### Universal CINO Priorities

1. **Building a repeatable innovation operating model — not running isolated projects.** The mature CINO agenda is explicitly a *system* agenda: stage-gate processes, portfolio governance, innovation accounting, BU-handoff mechanisms, kill criteria, resource-reallocation rhythms. Too little structure and efforts dissipate; too much and bureaucracy strangles creativity. The 2024–26 reflex is toward *more* structure — the "let a thousand flowers bloom" era gave way to portfolio discipline because boards are tired of pilot-purgatory war stories. Anything that adds process bloat without improving throughput swims against the tide.

2. **Emerging-tech sensing and evaluation at compressed speed.** GenAI made the 18-month tech-evaluation cycle untenable — a state-of-the-art foundation model in January is superseded by June. CINOs are building faster sensing: always-on scanning of patent filings, arXiv, startup funding, VC portfolios, academic labs, competitor actions. The technology radar is no longer a quarterly artifact; it's a living system. CINOs extend the radar beyond AI — quantum, synthetic biology, advanced materials, XR, robotics — because the next foundational shift may not come from the obvious place.

3. **Corporate venture building and ecosystem participation as table stakes.** CVC has become baseline capability in FSI, healthcare, industrial, telecom, CPG. CVC serves three purposes simultaneously: strategic intelligence (see what startups are building before they threaten the core), option value (first look at acquisition targets), innovation-pipeline seeding. Running credible CVC requires real check-sizes, VC-comparable speed, and a post-investment support model — all organizational design the CINO must personally architect.

4. **Solving the "valley of death" between pilot and production.** The 2024–26 reality check: boards stopped asking "are we innovating?" and started asking "why haven't your innovations scaled?" Successful experiments that can't cross the handoff — because BU leaders won't adopt, CIO architecture won't accept, CFO budget won't fund — represent the largest source of CINO frustration and the largest driver of CINO replacement. The 2025 agenda is explicitly **lab-to-production engineering**: enterprise-ready architecture from day one, governance pre-approved with CIO/CISO, BU co-investment at pilot stage, named scaling officers with formal authority.

5. **Defining what AI makes *newly possible* — not just how to deploy AI.** The single most strategically distinctive CINO question in 2025–26. The CIO's question is operational (deploy at scale, safely, on existing infrastructure). The CAIO/CAO's question is capabilities-building (build and govern models). The CINO's question is *business-model-native*: what entirely new value propositions, channels, and monetization models does AI enable that weren't economically viable before? AI-native customer advisors, autonomous service delivery, hyper-personalized products at scale, agent-to-agent commerce — these are CINO-altitude questions that sit above the CIO's deployment agenda.

6. **Building innovation culture and intrapreneurship capability.** Design-thinking training, hackathons, rotation programs, intrapreneur tracks, recognition systems, and failure-tolerance norms that shift the org from "perfect plan before start" to "hypothesis and fast experiment." Culture is the slowest part of the CINO's portfolio and the part most vulnerable to CEO turnover or budget pressure. The 2025 priority is making culture *structural* — hard-wiring experimentation into performance-review criteria, promotion pathways, and budget cycles — so it survives leadership change.

7. **Strategic foresight and scenario planning against disruption vectors.** Five-to-fifteen-year industry-evolution models, pressure-tested against the current innovation portfolio: *"If [AI disrupts banking / GLP-1 reshapes CPG / fusion becomes viable / autonomous reshapes logistics] unfolds, are we investing in the right things?"* Boards are increasingly asking CINOs to present these scenarios directly — a 2024–26 shift that has elevated the CINO's strategic profile but also raised the performance bar.

8. **Competitive intelligence on innovator peers.** CINOs benchmark obsessively against peer CINOs at innovator companies (not average competitors). Who is launching what CVC fund? Who hired which ex-founder? Which university partnership went exclusive? What did the competitor's innovation-day announce? This peer-benchmarking shapes the CINO's own agenda more than any internal strategy document — and it means vendors who can credibly reference what innovator peers are doing carry disproportionate weight.

### The Universal Scoreboard: Innovation-Revenue % and Portfolio Velocity

Across every industry in the research, CINOs volunteer two headline metrics more often than anything else: **percentage of revenue from offerings launched in the last 3–5 years (the "Vitality Index")** and **time from concept to validated evidence (portfolio velocity)**. The first is what the board is measured on by analysts and activists; the second is what the CINO controls operationally. Revenue is the CEO's number; EBIT is the CFO's; these two are *the* CINO numbers. If you cannot draw a credible line from your solution to moving one of them, you are not speaking the CINO's native tongue.

### Universal CINO KPIs

| KPI | What It Signals | Why CINOs Care |
|-----|----------------|---------------|
| **Vitality Index (% revenue from last 3–5 years' launches)** | New-offering commercial success at scale | The board's single cleanest "is the innovation system working?" metric; typically targeted at 20–35% |
| **Portfolio velocity (concept-to-evidence cycle time)** | Speed of the build-measure-learn loop | The CINO's operational lever; target is often under 90 days concept-to-first-evidence |
| **Pilot-to-production conversion rate** | Whether innovations actually cross the valley of death | The single most-watched 2025 metric; industry average estimated at 10–20% |
| **Portfolio allocation (H1/H2/H3 split)** | Is risk appetite calibrated, or has portfolio collapsed into H1? | Typical targets 70/20/10 or 60/25/15; drift toward H1 signals innovation-theater risk |
| **Kill rate at stage gates** | Is the team making real bets or protecting pet projects? | Healthy kill rates typically 50–70% at early stages; low kill rates signal insufficient risk or weak evaluation |
| **BU adoption of innovation outputs** | Are innovations actually landing in the core? | The anti-pilot-purgatory metric; typically tracked as # of innovations adopted into BU P&L per year |
| **Ecosystem engagement (CVC deployments, partnerships, LOIs)** | Is the CINO a credible ecosystem player? | CVC check count + value, partnership count, patent / JV / LOI volume |
| **Innovation team retention & attraction** | Is the CINO winning the talent war with startups? | Retention of critical roles, hiring against startup comp, named ex-founders joining the team |

### What CINOs Privately Grade Themselves On

**What this means (TL;DR).** The KPI table above is what the CINO reports to the CEO/Board. What they *actually* grade themselves on — the internal scorecard — is a different and broader list. These are the metrics that show up in the CINO's head during a Sunday portfolio review.

**Why it's CINO-specific.** An R&D head grades themselves on technical milestones. A CSO grades themselves on strategic narrative quality. The CINO alone grades themselves on the *system* — did the whole innovation apparatus produce winners that scaled, without the team leaving, without the core rejecting, with ecosystem credibility intact?

**How to use this scorecard (field rule).** Before any CINO meeting, identify which **one or two items** on this list the CINO is *privately most anxious about* right now — based on innovation-day presentations, public portfolio updates, recent team departures, recent CVC activity. Then match your pitch to that anxiety, not to a generic value prop.

#### 1. Pilot-to-production conversion rate

- **What it means.** Of the experiments that pass stage gates and are deemed successful at the lab/pilot level, what percentage actually land in the core P&L within 12–18 months?
- **Why CINOs care specifically.** This is the single most visible failure mode of the role. A CINO with a 100-pilot portfolio and a 5% conversion rate is a CINO about to be replaced. A CINO with a 40-pilot portfolio and a 30% conversion rate is a CINO getting a larger budget.
- **Vocabulary.** "Conversion rate," "landed in the P&L," "BU-adopted," "scaled into the core," "valley-of-death crossing."
- **Can your solution move this?** **Yes if** you explicitly address the scaling pathway — enterprise-architecture compatibility, CIO/CISO pre-validation, BU-leader co-investment mechanisms, deployment-automation across business units. **No unless** your pitch names the specific friction points that kill conversions.

#### 2. The "named moonshot" — does it still look credible?

- **What it means.** Every CINO has 1–3 signature bets that the CEO has personally referenced publicly. The CINO is graded on whether those specific bets still look credible 12–24 months in.
- **Why CINOs care.** A named moonshot that visibly dies kills the CINO's credibility in a single board cycle. Conversely, a named moonshot that hits a visible milestone (first patent, first customer LOI, first revenue) buys the CINO years of runway.
- **Vocabulary.** The specific bet's name (e.g., "Project Aurora," "the parametric venture," "the AI-advisory launch").
- **Can your solution move this?** **Yes if** you tie directly to the named bet by name, with a mechanism that compresses a specific critical-path milestone. **No unless** you've identified the bet from public sources and can name it.

#### 3. Ecosystem credibility — are VCs and founders taking the CINO's calls?

- **What it means.** When a hot startup is raising, do they take a meeting with the CINO? Do top-tier VCs share dealflow? Do universities offer exclusive research partnerships? This is a social-capital metric that compounds or erodes quietly.
- **Why CINOs care.** Ecosystem credibility is the CINO's external moat. Once lost, it takes years to rebuild — startups, VCs, and universities talk, and "tourist corporate CINO" is a hard label to shed.
- **Vocabulary.** "Dealflow," "our ecosystem position," "the founders we work with," "our LP relationships," "first look."
- **Can your solution move this?** **Yes if** you bring credible ecosystem access (VC intros, startup dealflow, university partnerships) or help the CINO engage with startups at startup-native speed. **No unless** your approach is ecosystem-sensitive — "come through our enterprise procurement process" fails.

#### 4. Innovation-team retention in critical roles

- **What it means.** Can the CINO retain the 5–15 critical roles (lab leads, venture principals, intrapreneur founders, data scientists, design leads) who carry the institutional knowledge and external relationships?
- **Why CINOs care.** Losing a single lab lead often kills the associated project. A wave of departures signals to the ecosystem that the CINO is losing; the next round of hires becomes harder.
- **Vocabulary.** "Bench," "we lost [name] to [startup]," "the team," "our founders-in-residence," "retention against comp."
- **Can your solution move this?** **Yes if** you genuinely make the team's work easier, faster, or higher-status. **No unless** the tool is something the team would have chosen themselves — CINO-imposed tools that the team resents accelerate attrition.

#### 5. Portfolio-allocation drift toward H1

- **What it means.** Under CFO and BU pressure, most CINOs face gravitational pull away from H2 and H3 into H1 (where ROI is easier to show). A CINO whose portfolio is 90% H1 has become a glorified applied-technology program.
- **Why CINOs care.** Portfolio drift is the quietest failure mode — it happens one quarter at a time, never visibly, and ends with the CINO role being absorbed into Strategy or IT.
- **Vocabulary.** "Protecting the H3 budget," "defending optionality," "real options," "our 70/20/10 target."
- **Can your solution move this?** **Yes if** your offering explicitly supports H2 or H3 bets with mechanisms that defend them against H1 gravity (e.g., dedicated exploration platforms, ecosystem-partnership tooling). **No unless** you position against H1 optimization tools.

#### 6. Speed advantage over peer CINOs

- **What it means.** Did the CINO get to a specific technology, partnership, or market position before their benchmarked peer CINOs at innovator companies?
- **Why CINOs care.** The innovator-peer benchmark is the CINO's implicit scorecard. "[Competitor CINO] announced their quantum program six months before us" is a CINO-career event.
- **Vocabulary.** "Ahead of [named peer company]," "first in our industry," "our first-mover position."
- **Can your solution move this?** **Yes if** you can credibly claim to give the CINO a timing advantage (early access, exclusive partnership, limited-cohort program). **No unless** you accept that "we serve your competitor too" is sometimes a disqualifier at the CINO level, even if it's an asset elsewhere.

#### 7. Board narrative quality

- **What it means.** Can the CINO walk into the next board meeting with a 10-minute story that holds up — compelling, evidenced, forward-looking, and consistent with previous quarters' narrative?
- **Why CINOs care.** The board deck is the CINO's twice-a-year performance review. A strong deck buys patience; a weak deck triggers scrutiny that compounds.
- **Vocabulary.** "The board narrative," "what I'm telling the board next month," "our innovation story."
- **Can your solution move this?** **Yes if** you explicitly help construct the narrative — peer proof, quantified results, forward-looking milestones, competitive positioning — that the CINO can re-tell to the board.

> **Tying a solution to one or two items on this private scorecard earns more CINO attention than tying it to the Vitality Index alone.** The sales agent's job: diagnose which private-scorecard item is most acute this quarter, then frame the pitch against it.

### Universal CINO Pain Points

- **The organizational immune system.** Governance, budget cycles, compliance frameworks, performance management, and cultural norms designed to protect the core business actively reject anything unfamiliar or unproven. The CINO is perpetually negotiating with the immune system rather than commanding it.
- **Scaling innovations into the core business (pilot purgatory).** The single biggest source of CINO frustration: innovations that work in the lab but can't integrate into enterprise systems, processes, and organization. Sales teams won't sell new products without compensation incentives; operations won't support unfamiliar service models; IT rejects non-standard architecture; CISO blocks non-compliant data flows.
- **Resource competition and portfolio overcommitment.** Running too many pilots with too few resources. Every new initiative competes with fifteen others for limited attention, budget, and team capacity. The CINO's hardest discipline is saying no — including to CEO-originated pet projects.
- **Innovation-budget vulnerability in downturns.** Innovation budgets are the first thing cut when earnings pressure intensifies, because returns are long-term and uncertain. The CINO's credibility determines whether the portfolio survives budget resets.
- **Timing uncertainty on emerging technology.** Too early means burning resources on immature tech or unready markets (the metaverse cycle, some quantum bets). Too late means entrenched competitors and lost access to talent and partners. The CINO is graded on timing judgment with minimal information.
- **Culture-change fragility.** Shifting from "perfect plan before start" to "hypothesis and fast experiment" requires sustained effort. Culture gains are easily reversed by a single leadership change, failed high-profile bet, or budget cut.
- **The flight-risk team.** Innovation teams attract people who could (and often do) start their own companies. Retention is structurally harder than in the core business; a single ex-founder principal leaving can kill a project.
- **Board-narrative volatility.** The same portfolio that looks brilliant in Q2 can look reckless in Q4 if a single visible bet falters. The CINO lives with board-sentiment volatility that the core business doesn't face.

### AI-Specific Pain Points

- **Distinguishing AI hype from substance at compressed speed.** GenAI capabilities evolve monthly; vendor demos wildly outpace production reality. The CINO needs evaluation frameworks that are both fast and rigorous — a combination few vendors enable.
- **Defining AI strategy vs. AI deployment.** The CIO owns deployment. The CAIO owns capability building. The CINO has to own "what new businesses AI enables" — but this question is so new that most enterprises haven't defined the boundary, and CINOs are fighting for the mandate against CIOs and CAIOs who want it.
- **AI pilot-to-production valley.** The general pilot-purgatory problem is acute for AI: governance, compliance (EU AI Act, state laws), data-residency, model-drift, and explainability requirements stall AI innovations at the handoff. CINOs need AI-specific scaling pathways, not generic ones.
- **Ecosystem competition against hyperscalers and AI-native startups.** The most interesting AI-native startups can choose whether to partner with an enterprise CINO or go direct to market / hyperscaler. The CINO's pitch to startups is competing with OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, AWS, and Azure partner programs.
- **Autonomous-agent accountability gap.** When an AI agent acts on behalf of the company, who is accountable? CINOs running agent experiments face unresolved questions from Legal, CISO, and Compliance — and "we'll figure it out later" is no longer acceptable in 2025.
- **Regulatory surface expansion.** EU AI Act phase-in through 2026–27, US state AI laws, sectoral AI rules (FDA, FINRA) — all add evaluation overhead to AI experiments and constrain where certain AI innovations can be built and run.

### 5.3 Universal AI Value Levers for CINOs

These are the seven ways AI creates value that CINOs care about — mapped directly to the CINO's Priorities (Section 2) and Private Scorecard (Section 3). For each lever, the agentic AI dimension shows how autonomous agents elevate the opportunity beyond traditional AI.

3. **Emerging technology + AI convergence.** Identifying where AI combines with other emerging technologies (quantum, biotech, spatial computing, IoT, robotics) to create breakthrough innovation opportunities. *Agentic dimension:* Technology convergence agents that monitor multiple emerging technology streams, identify intersection opportunities, model potential business impact, and generate innovation briefs — expanding the CINO's peripheral vision.

4. **Innovation portfolio management.** AI that helps manage the innovation portfolio — balancing incremental, adjacent, and transformational bets; predicting which experiments will succeed; and optimizing resource allocation. *Agentic dimension:* Portfolio intelligence agents that track experiment progress, predict success probability based on early signals, recommend resource reallocation, and generate portfolio dashboards — making innovation investment decisions more rigorous.

6. **Innovation culture & methods.** Using AI to democratize innovation methodology — enabling any team in the enterprise to run structured innovation processes without dedicated innovation facilitators. *Agentic dimension:* Innovation coaching agents that guide business teams through design thinking, lean startup, or other methodologies — scaling innovation methods beyond the CINO's team capacity.

7. **Business model innovation with AI.** Identifying how AI enables fundamentally new business models — subscription services, data monetization, platform plays, and AI-as-a-service opportunities. *Agentic dimension:* Business model agents that analyze enterprise capabilities, market dynamics, and AI possibilities to identify new business model opportunities — generating innovation proposals for adjacent/transformational growth.

### 5.4 Quality Bar: How CINOs Filter AI Pitches

CINOs are open-minded and forward-looking — they want to see the art of the possible. But they also need credibility, which means connecting innovation to business outcomes. The pattern across innovation functions is identical — CINOs take AI seriously when it passes four innovation tests simultaneously:

1. **Novel enough to justify the innovation function's involvement.** If it's incremental process automation, it belongs with the CIO. The CINO needs something genuinely new — new business models, new capabilities, new market opportunities that don't fit existing functional boundaries.
2. **Demonstrable within 90 days.** The CINO lives by proof-of-concept speed. If it takes 12 months to show anything, the CINO's stakeholders will lose patience. Rapid prototyping capability is essential.
3. **Scalable beyond the lab.** The CINO has been burned by innovations that work in the lab and die in production. Show the path from experiment to enterprise — including data requirements, integration needs, and operational considerations.
4. **Connects to a business outcome the CEO cares about.** The CINO needs to justify their function's existence. Innovation for its own sake is dead. Connect every AI innovation to revenue growth, cost reduction, customer experience, or strategic positioning.

**Field rule:** If a CINO-level pitch cannot check all four — novel, rapid-demo, scalable, business-connected — it reads as either too incremental (give it to CIO) or too blue-sky (no credibility path). Lead with the novel business opportunity and the rapid demonstration path. When generating Call Plan Section 4, ensure every AI story includes: (a) what's genuinely novel, (b) how fast a POC can be built, (c) the scaling path from lab to enterprise, (d) which CEO-level business outcome it serves.

## 7. Technology Evaluation Style

CINOs evaluate technology differently from every other C-suite executive. They are far more willing to run pilots, proofs-of-concept, and small-scale experiments before committing — and far less patient with traditional enterprise procurement processes. Their key questions are:

- *"Can I test this in weeks, not months?"*
- *"What becomes newly possible with this — not just 'what improves'?"*
- *"How fast is the vendor improving, and are they aligned with where the market is heading?"*
- *"Is this differentiated enough to warrant a portfolio slot?"*
- *"Does it pre-solve the lab-to-production handoff, or will it stall at scale?"*

They rely on their innovation team, technology radar, and ecosystem network to vet technical decisions — but the final filter is *portfolio fit* and *trajectory judgment.* **Don't pitch current-state features to a CINO — pitch trajectory, novelty, and what becomes newly possible.** And don't pitch rigid enterprise commitments — CINOs buy options, not contracts.

### When the CINO Engages Directly

- **Core-business AI deployment** (goes to CAIO or CIO)

### Multi-Stakeholder Dynamics

Getting CINO sponsorship is the fastest enterprise entry point for emerging vendors — but CINO-only deals stall at the pilot stage without broader enterprise validation. The ideal sequence:

1. **Enter through the CINO or innovation team** — low-commitment pilot, clear success criteria, 60–90 day timeline.
2. **Prove the pilot with explicit evidence** — the evidence must be credible to CIO, CISO, and the target BU leader, not just the innovation team.
3. **Pre-engage CIO, CISO, Compliance during the pilot** — so scaling decisions don't stall at the handoff.
4. **Identify the receiving BU leader early** — their co-investment during the pilot dramatically increases scaling probability.
5. **Build the scaling business case with CFO and target BU leader** — not just the CINO.
6. **Scale with CINO + CIO + BU leader co-sponsorship** — the CINO provides innovation-narrative cover; the BU owns the P&L outcome; the CIO owns the technical integration.

> **Critical warning:** Vendors who can only sell to innovation teams — without navigating the scaling handoff — end up stuck in perpetual pilot mode. The CINO is a valuable *first door*, not a final buyer for enterprise deployment. Plan the multi-stakeholder path from day one.

| 2 | **"We're already running 15 pilots — why add another?"** | Portfolio fit. The CINO is rationalizing, not expanding. | *"This specifically fills your [portfolio gap] that your current experiments don't address. It addresses [named private-scorecard priority — e.g., your H3 agent-banking bet / your named moonshot]."* |
| 3 | **"What happens if you're not around in two years?"** | Vendor risk on a trajectory bet. CINOs know innovation vendors are often fragile. | *"Funding runway through [date]; [N] enterprise customers in production; open standards and portable data; [specific continuity mechanism]. Here's how [named peer] mitigated vendor-risk on a comparable bet."* |
| 4 | **"How does this connect to our innovation thesis?"** | Strategic-narrative fit. Can the CINO re-tell this to the board? | *"Based on your [published thesis / innovation-day slide / earnings reference], you're betting on [theme]. This directly advances that by [specific mechanism]. Here's the one-slide board-ready narrative."* |
| 5 | **"Can we start small and fast?"** | Portfolio discipline. CINOs buy options, not commitments. | *"Two-week sandbox, $[X] commitment, [specific success criteria]. If it works, we build from there. If not, you've lost [minimal amount] and learned [specific hypothesis]."* |

> **Archetype weighting:**
> - **Visionary** → Leads with #4 (thesis fit) and #2 (portfolio differentiation). Wants novelty and ambition.
> - **Portfolio Manager** → Leads with #2 (portfolio fit) and #5 (stage-gate structure). Wants rigor.
> - **Venture Builder** → Leads with #3 (vendor resilience) and #4 (ecosystem fit). Thinks in startup risk.
> - **Scaler** → Leads with #1 (scaling path). Anything without a scaling answer dies first.
> - **Defender** → Leads with #5 (speed) and #2 (differentiation from competitor responses). Urgency dominates.

#### Objection 1 — "How does this scale into the core business?"

- **Literal phrasings.** *"What happens at the handoff?" / "How do we move from pilot to production?" / "Will our CIO/CISO accept this at scale?" / "Has anyone actually scaled this into a BU's P&L?"*
- **What they're really asking.** "My biggest failure mode is pilot purgatory. I need to know before I authorize the pilot that the scaling path exists — because if it doesn't, I'm buying another dead-end experiment."
- **How to answer (template).** *"Scaling pathway is pre-engineered: architecture is [enterprise-ready / cloud-native / API-first]; CIO/CISO pre-validation takes [N weeks] based on [peer reference]; BU co-investment model is [specific mechanism]. [Named peer CINO] scaled this from innovation-lab pilot to deployment across [N] business units in [timeframe]. Their Head of Innovation will take a 45-minute call to walk your CIO and target BU leader through the handoff."*
- **What NOT to say.** "Scaling is the customer's responsibility." "We'll figure out scaling after the pilot." "Our enterprise customers love this." (Vague.) Never treat the scaling question as a post-pilot problem.
- **Worked example.** A pitch for a GenAI experimentation platform to a bank CINO: *"The platform runs on [AWS Bedrock / Azure OpenAI / your approved cloud]. We've pre-engineered the CISO-audit package — [named peer bank's] CISO approved it in 6 weeks based on our SOC 2 Type II and data-residency controls. BU co-investment: your Wealth Management P&L owner contributes 25% of the pilot budget and gets first access to the output; this is the exact model [named bank] used to scale their AI advisory pilot from the innovation lab to production across 1,400 advisors in 9 months. Their Chief Innovation Officer will take your team through the handoff playbook."*

#### Objection 2 — "We're already running 15 pilots — why add another?"

- **Literal phrasings.** *"We have enough pilots running." / "Show me how this is different from our current experiments." / "Why does this warrant a portfolio slot?"*
- **What they're really asking.** "My biggest discipline is saying no. Convince me this fills a gap my current portfolio doesn't address — or I'm killing it to protect my team's bandwidth."
- **How to answer (template).** *"This fills a specific gap in your current portfolio: [named gap — e.g., H3 optionality on agent-mediated banking; H2 new-venture in embedded finance]. Your current experiments address [X, Y, Z] but not [the specific thesis]. It directly advances your [named private-scorecard priority]. [Named peer CINO] faced the same portfolio-overcommitment question and filled exactly this gap; here's how they rationalized."*
- **What NOT to say.** "We offer unique value." (Adjective without proof.) "This is differentiated." (Without specifying what it's differentiated from.) "You'll find a way to fit it in." (Dismisses the real constraint.)
- **Worked example.** A pitch to a pharma CINO: *"Your current AI-drug-discovery portfolio includes the Insilico partnership on fibrosis and the internal ML team on oncology pipeline. Neither addresses foundation models for protein-language understanding — the H3 bet where [named peer pharma] is already six months ahead via their DeepMind partnership. This specifically fills that gap. It's a $400K experiment over 90 days, with explicit kill criteria — and it gives you a protein-language-model data asset even if the hypothesis fails."*

#### Objection 3 — "What happens if you're not around in two years?"

- **Literal phrasings.** *"What's your runway?" / "What if you get acquired?" / "What happens to our data if you shut down?" / "Why shouldn't we just build this ourselves?"*
- **What they're really asking.** "I'm betting on an emerging company to help me make a trajectory bet. I need to know the bet has resilience — not that I'm compounding my own innovation risk with vendor risk."
- **How to answer (template).** *"Funding: [Series X, $Y raised, runway through Z]. Customers: [N] enterprise customers in production with [industry mix]. Continuity: open standards, documented APIs, portable data, [specific continuity mechanism like source-code escrow or data-export guarantees]. Acquisition-protection: our top investors have explicit [terms]. Here's how [named peer CINO] mitigated vendor-risk on a comparable emerging-tech bet — we applied the same model to our contract with you."*
- **What NOT to say.** "We're growing fast." (Without numbers.) "We're not going anywhere." (Meaningless.) "Our investors love us." (Irrelevant to the risk question.)
- **Worked example.** A pitch to an energy CINO on a fusion-adjacent simulation platform: *"Series B, $32M raised, runway through Q2 2027. Seven enterprise customers in production including [named oil major] and [named utility]. Our data format is open — HDF5 + documented schema. Source-code escrow in place with Iron Mountain. Our investors include [strategic investor aligned with your sector], which gives us a strategic-alignment mechanism you can leverage. [Named peer energy CINO] structured their contract with us to include data-portability guarantees and an acquisition-standstill clause — we'll offer you the same."*

#### Objection 4 — "How does this connect to our innovation thesis?"

- **Literal phrasings.** *"Show me how this fits our published strategy." / "Where does this sit in our portfolio?" / "What does this do for our board narrative?"*
- **What they're really asking.** "Even if I like this, I have to retell it to the CEO and Board. Give me the narrative that fits my innovation thesis — or I can't sell it internally."
- **How to answer (template).** *"Your published innovation thesis — from your [innovation-day deck / annual report / CEO letter] — names [three themes]. This specifically advances [theme X] by [mechanism]. Here's a one-slide board-ready narrative: (1) this thesis / (2) current-state gap / (3) this experiment's hypothesis / (4) 90-day evidence milestone / (5) scaling trigger. Your board has already seen [named peer CINO] present exactly this kind of structured-bet slide; we can pattern yours on it."*
- **What NOT to say.** "We'll figure out the positioning later." "This is strategic for anyone." (Generic.) Never make the CINO do the narrative construction.
- **Worked example.** A pitch to a CPG CINO: *"Your innovation thesis published at the last investor day names 'GLP-1-aware portfolio reshaping,' 'D2C at scale,' and 'AI-native brand experiences.' This experiment specifically advances the third — AI-powered personalized shopping agents for your top three brands. One-slide board story: (1) AI-native brand experiences thesis / (2) current-state gap: no personalized-agent capability / (3) hypothesis: conversion uplift of >15% on [named brand] / (4) 90-day evidence milestone: live agent deployed with 5K users / (5) scaling trigger: if CVR >15%, scale to remaining priority brands in H2 2026. Your CEO can reference this at the next analyst day."*

#### Objection 5 — "Can we start small and fast?"

- **Literal phrasings.** *"What's the smallest commitment?" / "Can we sandbox this?" / "How fast can we start?" / "I don't want to commit to a big contract yet."*
- **What they're really asking.** "I want to test this without an enterprise commitment. If you can't match my velocity, you're the wrong vendor."
- **How to answer (template).** *"Two-week sandbox: $[X] commitment, [specific scope], [specific success criteria]. If the 90-day experiment validates, we extend into a structured pilot at $[Y]. If it doesn't, you've spent $[X] and learned [hypothesis]. No multi-year commitment required before evidence. Here's the pre-built sandbox agreement — your legal team has seen this format before from [named peer vendor]."*
- **What NOT to say.** "Our standard enterprise agreement is..." (Wrong meeting.) "We can customize." (Evasive.) "Let's talk to procurement." (Fails the speed test.)
- **Worked example.** A pitch to an automotive CINO: *"Two-week sandbox: $75K commitment, deploys our simulation platform against your ADAS edge-case dataset. Success criteria: identify 20% more edge cases than your current validation pipeline, verified by your autonomy team. If yes, we extend into a 90-day experiment at $350K covering three sensor configurations. If no, you've spent $75K and have a benchmark of your current pipeline's performance. Our master-sandbox-agreement template has been pre-cleared by [named peer auto OEM's] legal team — we can send it today."*

> **Field rule:** Show up with pre-built answers to all five. Hand the CINO a physical one-slide "innovation-thesis fit" deck at the end of the meeting — the portfolio position, the peer reference, the 90-day evidence milestone, the scaling trigger, the vendor-resilience framework. That leave-behind is the most valuable artifact in a CINO sale, because it's the narrative the CINO uses to defend the decision in their next portfolio review.

**Common misreads.**

- **NOT negotiation objections.** Negotiation is price and terms. The five CINO objections are about *narrative sufficiency and portfolio fit.*
- **NOT asked in strict sequence.** A CINO may open with #4 (thesis fit), pivot to #1 (scaling), close with #5 (start small). Be ready in any order.
- **"No objection" is not agreement.** A CINO who asks none of the five has likely already delegated the decision to the innovation team lead — and the pilot will run, but the scaling deal won't.

### Organizational Politics to Navigate

| Dynamic | What's Happening | How to Navigate |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **CINO vs. CIO** | CINO's speed vs. CIO's architecture standards; "shadow IT" accusations | Bring CIO into evaluation early; show CIO-ready architecture from day one |
| **CINO vs. CFO** | Patient capital vs. measurable returns; innovation-accounting vs. standard ROI | Frame as real options; show stage-gate discipline; be transparent about failures |
| **CINO vs. CAIO** | Unresolved territorial question: who owns "AI strategy"? | Don't take sides; position to serve both; address AI deployment (CAIO) and AI business models (CINO) |
| **CINO vs. Business Unit Leaders** | Innovation portfolio sometimes bypasses BU priorities; BU leaders protect P&L | Co-develop with BU leaders from the start; name the target BU in the pitch |
| **CINO vs. Chief Strategy Officer** | Innovation strategy vs. corporate strategy — who informs whom? | Frame as complementary: strategy pulls innovation toward priorities; innovation pushes strategy to new possibilities |
| **CINO navigating board activists / strategic reviews** | When activists push for focus, innovation budgets are first targets | Help CINO build the narrative that innovation is a value lever, not a cost center |

> **Critical insight:** The **CIO and CFO** can kill any CINO-sponsored scaling decision by raising architecture or ROI concerns. Include them early — ideally during the pilot, not after it concludes.

Select 3–5 questions based on the CINO's archetype, the current sales stage, and what you already know from the Engagement Plan. Do NOT use all questions in one meeting — a CINO meeting is 30–45 minutes.*

### 🇨🇳 中国CINO Discovery Questions

| 问题 | 目的 |
|------|------|

| "有跟高校/研究所合作吗？" | 产学研协同 |

### 全球CINO参考（Global Reference）

### Universal Questions (work with any archetype)

1. "What's your innovation thesis for the next 3–5 years — where are you placing your biggest bets on how your industry will change?"
2. "When you look at your current innovation portfolio, where are you seeing the most promising traction — and where are experiments stalling?"
3. "What's the hardest part of moving a successful innovation from your lab into the core business? Where does it typically get stuck?"
4. "How are you evaluating AI's potential to create entirely new business models, versus improving existing operations?"

### Archetype-Adapted Questions

**For Visionaries** (frontier, novelty, possibility):
- "What technology or behavioral shift, if it played out faster than expected, would most reshape your industry in the next 5–10 years?"
- "Where are you intentionally placing bets that could look foolish today but defining tomorrow?"

**For Portfolio Managers** (stage-gate, allocation, discipline):
- "How is your H1/H2/H3 allocation trending versus your target — and where are you feeling allocation drift?"
- "What's your current kill rate at early stage-gates, and do you think it's rigorous enough?"

**For Venture Builders** (CVC, startups, ecosystem):
- "How is your CVC deal-flow quality trending? Which sectors are you getting the most differentiated looks in?"
- "Which startup relationships have moved from CVC investment into genuine strategic partnerships, and what's been the pattern?"

**For Scalers** (lab-to-production, BU adoption):
- "What's your current pilot-to-production conversion rate, and what mechanisms are you experimenting with to improve it?"
- "Which BU leader has been the most effective partner in scaling innovations into their P&L, and what's the pattern?"

**For Defenders** (disruption, competitive threat):
- "Which disruptor or emerging competitor do you consider the most acute threat to your core business over the next 3 years?"
- "Where are you deliberately defensive — and where are you choosing to let someone else go first?"

### Stage-Adapted Questions

**Prospect stage** (first meeting):
- "What triggered your interest in this area now — a competitive move, a board ask, a technology breakthrough?"
- "How does your innovation team typically evaluate new technology — through challenges, cohort programs, or direct scouting?"

**Qualified / Technical Validation:**
- "When you look at your innovation investments over the past 3 years, where have you seen the best portfolio return — and where have you been disappointed?"
- "What would 'success' look like for this experiment — what evidence would you need to decide to scale?"

**Business Validation / Committed:**
- "What's the one remaining concern that, if resolved, would let us start the sandbox next week?"
- "Who else on your team — CIO, CISO, target BU leader, CFO — should we bring into the next conversation to pre-validate the scaling path?"

## 11. Do's & Don'ts

### 🇨🇳 中国CINO Do's & Don'ts

#### Do's ✅
| 规则 | 原因 |
|------|------|
| 展示前沿性/差异化 | CINO最open |
| 提供快速POC能力 | "先试"文化 |
| 讲生态(产学研/合作) | CINO看生态 |

#### Don'ts ❌
| 禁忌 | 原因 |
|------|------|
| 太成熟/太大众 | CINO要前沿 |
| 不能POC/只能买 | CINO要试 |
| IP/知识产权不清 | 创新很在意IP |

### 全球CINO参考（Global Reference）

### ✅ DO

- **Lead with what becomes newly possible, not what improves.** CINOs are allergic to incremental framing. Open with "here's a capability or business model that didn't exist before — and here's how to test it in weeks."
- **Strip adjectives. Use trajectory, named peers, and ecosystem signals.** "Revolutionary," "next-generation," "disruptive" without substance reads as marketing. A CINO hears it as filler.
- **Name the horizon you target.** "This is a horizon-2 bet that de-risks your horizon-3 optionality" is a CINO-grade opening. It signals you understand the operating model.
- **Reference a named peer CINO or innovator company already running this.** "Peer CINO X at Innovator Y launched this program 8 months ago" is peer-benchmarked credibility — the currency CINOs trade in.
- **Pre-answer the five objections.** Scaling path, portfolio fit, vendor resilience, thesis fit, fast-start. If any is missing, the deal stalls.
- **Hand them the one-slide thesis-fit story.** Every CINO deal requires a narrative that fits the published innovation thesis and can be re-told to the board. Build the slide.
- **Offer low-commitment entry points.** Two-week sandbox, $50K–$250K pilot, clear success criteria, documented kill points. Rigid multi-year contracts signal you don't understand the role.
- **Show trajectory, not just current state.** Where is your technology heading? Where is your company heading? CINOs buy on trajectory judgment.
- **Engage the broader stakeholder map early.** CIO, CISO, CFO, target BU leader — bring them in during the pilot, not at the scaling decision. Surprise kills scaling deals.
- **Be transparent about maturity.** "This works today for X; Y is coming in Q2; Z is experimental" earns credibility. Over-polished claims destroy it.
- **Understand the published innovation thesis before pitching.** Innovation-day decks, annual-report innovation sections, CEO letters, and analyst-day transcripts are all public. Skipping them is unforgivable.

### ❌ DON'T

- **Don't pitch incremental improvement.** "15% faster" or "slightly better" belongs in the CIO's or COO's meeting. The CINO will disengage.
- **Don't use rigid enterprise-implementation language.** "12-month rollout," "multi-year commitment," "enterprise-wide deployment required" are disqualifying at the CINO level.
- **Don't over-polish the risk story.** "Zero risk," "guaranteed," "proven at scale" read as marketing. CINOs live in uncertainty; pretending otherwise signals you don't understand the seat.
- **Don't use me-too positioning.** "We do what [established vendor] does but better" is a horizon-1 framing that bores the CINO. Position against trajectory, not against incumbents.
- **Don't treat the CINO as a side door to bypass CIO governance.** Going around creates enemies. The CIO will notice and kill the scaling deal.
- **Don't lose patience during the incubation period.** CINO deals often involve 6–12 months of pilot learning before scaling decisions. Vendors who push for immediate scaling often lose the deal.
- **Don't ignore the published thesis.** Pitching without reading the innovation-day deck signals you don't take the CINO seriously.
- **Don't pitch without an ecosystem signal.** CINOs trust ecosystem validation (startup endorsement, VC reference, innovator-peer adoption) more than traditional enterprise logos. Bring at least one ecosystem proof point.
- **Don't confuse the CINO's question with the CIO's or CAIO's.** *"How do we deploy AI?"* is the CIO's question. *"How do we build AI capability?"* is the CAIO's. *"What new businesses does AI enable?"* is the CINO's. Confusing them is disqualifying.
- **Don't assume the same pitch works across archetypes.** A Visionary needs different framing than a Scaler. Diagnose the dominant posture first.

### Archetype-Specific Do's

| Archetype | Do This | Because |
|----------|---------|---------|
| **Visionary** | Lead with the 5-year possibility space; bring founders / ex-founders as references | They buy trajectory and novelty |
| **Portfolio Manager** | Show stage-gate rigor, innovation-accounting, explicit kill criteria | They buy system, not single bets |
| **Venture Builder** | Lead with startup partnerships, CVC synergy, founder relationships | They buy ecosystem position |
| **Scaler** | Lead with production references, scaling playbook, CIO/CISO pre-validation | They buy valley-of-death crossing |
| **Defender** | Lead with competitor-tracking, 90-day defensive milestones, speed benchmarks | They buy competitive response velocity |
